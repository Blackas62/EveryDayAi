Most of us over sixty have spent a lifetime learning new things the hard way, picking up skills on the job, raising families, navigating whatever technology landed in our laps decade by decade. AI is just the latest thing to land, and the good news is that it is genuinely one of the more forgiving technologies to pick up. You do not need a course, a qualification, or a grandchild on speed dial. You just need to know where it actually helps, and then give it a go.

I have been using AI tools every single day since late 2022, and the five areas below are where I see the clearest, most immediate benefit for people in our age group. None of this requires you to be technical. All of it is available right now, mostly for free or very low cost, on the phone or laptop you already own.

The first and perhaps most reassuring place to start is health. Not replacing your GP, let me be clear about that, but using AI to help you walk into that appointment better prepared. When a doctor mentions a medication, a test result, or a condition you have never heard of, you can come home and ask an AI to explain it in plain English, ask what questions you should have asked, or find out what lifestyle changes are commonly recommended alongside the treatment. I do this regularly. It turns a ten-minute appointment into something I actually understand and can act on. The AI will always tell you to follow up with your doctor for anything specific, and that is exactly right, but arriving informed is half the battle.

The second area is staying connected with family, particularly the grandchildren or younger relatives who seem to communicate in a dialect all their own. AI can help you write a message, draft a longer email, or even help you understand something a teenager sent you that made no sense at all. Beyond family, AI is genuinely good at helping you write a thank-you note, a speech for a birthday or memorial, a letter of complaint, or anything else where the blank page feels a bit daunting. You describe what you want to say, and the AI gives you a first draft you can shape into your own words. It takes the pressure off completely.

Learning new things is the third area, and this one quietly surprises people. If you have always wanted to understand how superannuation actually works, how to read a wine label with some confidence, what really happened during a particular period of history, or why your roses keep getting black spot, you can just ask. An AI like ChatGPT will explain things at whatever level you need, and you can keep asking follow-up questions until it clicks. There are no silly questions, no impatient sighs, and no need to feel embarrassed about not knowing something. I find myself learning something new almost every day through exactly this kind of conversation.

Managing finances and paperwork is the fourth area, and for many of us this has become more complex, not less, as we get older. Pension arrangements, private health insurance reviews, aged care options, estate planning basics, understanding what a letter from the ATO is actually asking, working out whether a quote for home repairs is in the right ballpark. AI cannot lodge your tax return or give you licensed financial advice, but it can help you understand documents, draft questions for your accountant or financial planner, compare options side by side in plain language, and spot when something in a letter or contract does not look quite right. That kind of clarity is worth a great deal.

The fifth area is creative hobbies, and this is the one that genuinely delights people once they discover it. If you paint, write, garden, cook, do family history research, make music, or follow any creative pursuit at all, AI becomes a thoughtful collaborator. Writers use it to break through a block or get feedback on a passage. Family history researchers use it to help interpret old documents or suggest where to look next. Gardeners ask it about companion planting or what is wrong with a struggling shrub. Cooks describe what is in the fridge and ask for a recipe idea. Quilters, knitters, woodworkers, and photographers all find uses. The key insight is that AI does not do the creative work for you, it works alongside you, and that is a very different and much more satisfying thing.

Before I give you a starting point, I want to share a few golden rules I always come back to, because they make the difference between using AI well and running into trouble. These are not meant to scare you off, quite the opposite. Following them means you can use AI with genuine confidence.

First, always fact-check anything important. AI is remarkably capable, but it can sometimes get things wrong, state an outdated figure, misremember a date, or present a plausible-sounding answer that does not hold up. For everyday questions this matters less, but if AI tells you something about your medication, your pension entitlements, or any decision with real consequences, take a moment to verify it with a reliable source or a qualified professional. Treat the AI's answer as a very good first draft, not the final word.

Second, never share sensitive personal details with an AI chat tool. That means no bank account numbers, no passwords, no Medicare card details, no tax file number, no credit card information. You simply do not need to share any of those things to get useful help, and sharing them creates an unnecessary risk. A good way to think about it: if you would not say it to a stranger on a bus who happened to be very knowledgeable, do not type it into a chat window.

Third, take a few minutes to look at the privacy settings of whatever AI tool you are using. Most reputable tools, including ChatGPT, give you options to control whether your conversations are used to improve the system. It is worth knowing what you have agreed to and adjusting the settings to suit your comfort level. You do not need to become a privacy expert, just the same basic caution you would apply to any app on your phone.

If none of the five areas has clicked into something concrete yet, here is the simplest possible starting point. Go to chat.openai.com on your phone or computer, create a free account, and type one question about something you genuinely want to know. Treat it like texting a very patient, very well-read friend. That is all it is. The rest follows naturally from there.
