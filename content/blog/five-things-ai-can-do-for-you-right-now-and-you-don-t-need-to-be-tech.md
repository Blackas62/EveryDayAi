Most people I talk to over sixty assume AI is for younger people, or for tech businesses, or for someone who has the time and patience to figure out complicated software. I've been using AI every single day since late 2022 and I can tell you honestly: that assumption is exactly backwards. The older you are, the more life experience you're bringing to the conversation, and AI turns out to be remarkably good at helping with the kinds of things that genuinely matter at this stage of life. Here are five of them.

The first is understanding medical information. You've just come out of a GP appointment and the doctor mentioned a condition, a medication, or a test result that you'd never heard of before. You didn't want to take up more of their time, so you nodded and walked out. AI is extraordinarily good at explaining medical terms and concepts in plain language, at whatever pace you need. You can type something like "explain what atrial fibrillation means in simple terms" and get a clear, readable answer in seconds. You can follow up with more questions. It is not a replacement for your doctor, and I'd never suggest treating it as one, but it is a genuinely brilliant way to arrive at your next appointment better informed and with better questions to ask.

The second is spotting scams before they get you. Scams targeting older Australians have become more sophisticated and more frequent. If you receive an email, a text message, or even a letter that feels a little off, you can paste the text straight into ChatGPT and ask: "does this look like a scam?" The tool is surprisingly good at identifying the red flags, things like unusual urgency, requests for personal details, suspicious links, and impersonation of government agencies. I have done this myself more times than I can count. It takes thirty seconds and gives you a second opinion from something that has read millions of examples of fraudulent communication. That alone can save you from a very bad day.

The third is writing letters and emails without the frustration. Plenty of people I know dread sitting down to write a formal letter, whether it's a complaint to a utility company, a letter to a strata manager, a submission to a council, or even a message to a family member about a sensitive topic. You don't have to get the words perfect before you go to AI. You can just tell it what you're trying to say, roughly and in your own words, and ask it to help you write something clear and professional. You stay in charge of the message; it just helps you find the right way to say it. The relief people feel when they discover this is real. Something that would have taken a stressful afternoon takes fifteen minutes.

The fourth is keeping your mind active with a genuinely engaging conversation partner. This one surprises people. AI can discuss history, books, current events, gardening, cooking, travel memories, philosophy, or just about anything else you're interested in, at depth, without rushing you and without losing the thread. For people who are living alone, or who have lost a spouse, or who simply want more intellectual stimulation than their day currently offers, this is not a trivial thing. I'm not suggesting AI replaces human connection, it doesn't and it shouldn't. But having something that will explore an idea with you at ten o'clock at night, when everyone else is asleep, has genuine value.

The fifth is making sense of paperwork. Tax documents, insurance policies, superannuation statements, rental agreements, aged care contracts: Australian bureaucratic paperwork is dense, often deliberately so. You can photograph a document or paste the text into an AI tool and ask it to explain what it actually means in plain English. Ask it which sections matter most. Ask it what questions you should be asking before you sign anything. This is not legal advice and I'd always say get a lawyer or financial adviser involved for anything significant, but AI can do a remarkable job of cutting through the language and helping you understand what you're looking at before you walk into that professional conversation.

None of this requires any technical background. You don't need to understand how AI works, any more than you need to understand how a car engine works in order to drive to the shops. What you do need is a willingness to try it once, properly, with a real question that matters to you. That first real use is usually the moment it clicks.
